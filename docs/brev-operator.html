01 Vision

The Gap

Brev today tracks goals, auto-generates check-ins, digests meetings, and surfaces risks. But tracking isn't operating. A real COO doesn't just report that a goal is at risk — they reassign work, escalate blockers, negotiate deadlines, and make sure the ball doesn't drop.

Brev Today

  • • Tracks goals & KPIs
  • • Auto-generates check-ins
  • • Digests meetings → cadences
  • • Surfaces risks & slippage
  • • Sends Slack notifications

Brev Operator

  • → Assigns work to the right people
  • → Follows up on commitments made in meetings
  • → Unblocks teams by routing decisions to decision-makers
  • → Reassigns work when someone is overloaded or stuck
  • → Prepares agendas, reports, and artifacts autonomously
  • → Escalates when timelines slip — with context, not just pings

"Don't tell me the goal is at risk. Tell me who needs to do what by when, and make sure they do it."

02 Why This Is a Brev Product

A standalone orchestrator (like the one in our previous plan) is a generic tool. Brev Operator is different because it has the full context graph:

🎯 Goals & OKRs

Who owns what. What's on track, at risk, behind. Key results with real-time metrics. The Operator knows what matters.

📅 Cadences

Meeting transcripts, action items, decisions made. The Operator knows what was promised and by whom.

👥 Org Graph

Teams, roles, reporting lines, workload. The Operator knows who's available, who's overloaded, who can decide.

📊 KPIs & Metrics

Auto-tracked from integrations. The Operator can see trends and act on them before humans notice.

🧠 Company Memory

Past decisions, rationale, context from months of meetings. The Operator doesn't forget why something was decided.

🔗 Integrations

GitHub, Linear, Jira, HubSpot, Google Workspace — the Operator has a full picture of execution state.

A generic Slack orchestrator would need to build all of this from scratch. Brev already has it. The Operator is the action layer on top of Brev's intelligence layer.

Orchestrator Brain

The decision-making core. Evaluates org state, detects what needs attention, decides what action to take and who should do it. Runs on a planning loop — not reactive, proactive.

Agent Pool

Isolated agents per org. Each agent has the org's full context but can't see other orgs. Spawned for specific tasks: drafting reports, researching blockers, preparing agendas.

Execution Engine

Tracks every commitment, follow-up, and deadline. Not a to-do list — a state machine that escalates, reassigns, and closes the loop.

04 Slack as a Two-Way Portal

The Operator in Slack isn't a chatbot. It's a portal into the full Brev platform.

Inbound: Slack → Brev

Create & Update

"@brev create a goal for Q2: increase NPS to 70" → goal created in Brev, assigned owner, linked to parent objective

Check-in

"@brev update ARR to $2.4M" → KPI updated, check-in logged, progress recalculated, status changed if threshold crossed

Assign & Delegate

"@brev assign the NPS goal to Sarah" → ownership transferred, Sarah notified in Slack with context

Query

"@brev what's at risk this week?" → real-time risk report with owners, blockers, and suggested actions

Outbound: Brev → Slack

Follow-ups

"Hey @jake, in yesterday's standup you mentioned the API migration would be done by Friday. How's it tracking?" — contextual, not nagging

Escalations

"@sarah, QA capacity is at 125% and blocking the V2 launch. Three options: (1) push V2 by a week, (2) bring in a contractor, (3) reduce scope. What's your call?"

Proactive Reports

Weekly digest dropped in #leadership: progress vs plan, risks, wins, team health — auto-generated, no human effort

Meeting Prep

Before a cadence meeting: agenda in thread, open action items, metrics since last review, suggested discussion topics

Key Principle: Not Just Pinging

❌ What notification bots do

"Reminder: Your OKR check-in is due"

"Reminder: Your OKR check-in is overdue"

"Reminder: Your OKR check-in is really overdue"

✅ What an AI COO does

"Hey @mike, I drafted your Q2 pipeline check-in based on HubSpot data and last week's forecast call. Pipeline is at $1.2M vs $1.5M target. I noted the Acme deal slipped — want me to flag that to Chris, or are you handling it? Here's the draft — reply 'approve' to submit, or edit inline."

06 Operator Agent Types

Inspired by OpenClaw's sessions_spawn model — each task gets an isolated agent with the right tools, context, and permissions. Multi-tenant: agents can only see their own org's data.

🧠 Orchestrator

The brain. Runs the planning loop. Evaluates org state, decides what needs attention, dispatches work agents.

Model: Opus/Sonnet · Runs: always-on per org

📝 Drafter

Writes check-ins, reports, agendas, digests. Pulls data from Brev APIs + integrations, produces artifacts.

Model: Sonnet · Runs: on-demand, spawned per task

🔎 Analyst

Deep-dives into data. Finds trends, correlations, blockers. Answers "why is this KPI declining?" with evidence.

Model: Sonnet/Opus · Runs: on-demand

📣 Coordinator

Handles human interactions in Slack. Follow-ups, approvals, question routing. Knows when to DM vs. thread vs. channel.

Model: Haiku/Sonnet · Runs: event-driven

⚡ Executor

Takes approved actions. Updates goals, reassigns owners, creates cadence events, modifies KPI targets.

Model: Haiku · Runs: on approval, short-lived

🔌 Integrator

Pulls data from external tools (HubSpot, Linear, Jira, Sheets). Reconciles external state with Brev's model.

Model: Haiku · Runs: scheduled + on-demand

07 Architectural Decisions

▶ ADR-1: Brev Extension vs. Standalone Product
Brev Extension
Brev ExtensionStandalone
ProsFull context graph (goals, cadences, users, memory). Existing auth, billing, multi-tenancy. Upsell path for current customers.Decoupled. Can serve non-Brev users. Simpler initial scope.
ConsCoupled to Brev's data model. Can't serve non-Brev users out of the box.No context. Must rebuild goal/OKR/cadence tracking. Commodity orchestrator.

The Operator's value IS the context. Without Brev's data, it's just another Slack bot. Build it as a first-class Brev feature.

▶ ADR-2: Multi-Tenant Agent Isolation
Per-Org Isolation
Per-Org IsolationShared Agent PoolSelf-Hosted (OpenClaw model)
ProsData isolation by design. Per-org context. SOC2 compliant.Efficient resource use. Simpler infra.Ultimate data sovereignty.
ConsMore compute. Per-org state management.Cross-contamination risk. Complex access control.Customer ops burden. Can't iterate centrally.

Each org gets isolated agent sessions. Agent tools are scoped to the org's data via Brev's existing auth/RLS. No org can see another org's goals, people, or conversations.

▶ ADR-3: Agent Runtime — Build vs. OpenClaw-Inspired
Custom, OpenClaw-Inspired

OpenClaw is single-tenant, self-hosted, single-process. We need multi-tenant, managed, horizontally scalable. But the concepts are gold:

Adopt from OpenClaw

  • • Session isolation model
  • • Sub-agent spawning pattern
  • • Skill/tool composition
  • • Cron-driven planning loops
  • • Memory architecture
  • • Question routing pattern

Build Custom

  • • Multi-tenant orchestration service
  • • Brev API tool layer
  • • Org-scoped agent state (Postgres, not SQLite)
  • • Horizontal scaling (workers, not single process)
  • • Slack app per-workspace install
  • • Usage metering & billing
▶ ADR-4: Human-in-the-Loop — Approval Model
Tiered Autonomy
Autonomous

Read data, draft artifacts, surface insights, send follow-up reminders.

Draft + Approve ★

Draft check-ins, propose reassignments, suggest escalations. Human approves via Slack reaction or reply.

Human Only

Delete goals, change owners across teams, modify org structure, adjust KPI targets.

The Operator should do the work and ask for approval, not ask humans to do the work. The output of every action is a draft, a recommendation, or a completed task — never a bare reminder.

▶ ADR-5: Slack Identity — Shared Bot vs. Dedicated Bot
Dedicated "Brev" Bot

Single Slack app: Brev. Installed per-workspace via OAuth. The Operator is Brev in Slack — same brand, same bot, same @mention. Users don't need to know about agents or orchestration. They just talk to Brev.

The Slack app needs: bot token scopes for messages/reactions/files/users, event subscriptions for mentions/reactions/thread replies, slash commands for quick queries.

08 Orchestration Loops

Loop 1: Proactive Planning (Cron — every 2–4 hours)

1

Scan org state. Read all goals, KPIs, check-ins, cadences. Identify: goals at risk, overdue check-ins, stale action items, approaching deadlines.

2

Prioritize. Rank by impact (company goal vs. team goal), urgency (days until deadline), and staleness (days since last update).

3

Plan actions. For each item: draft a check-in, compose a follow-up, prepare an escalation, or schedule a deep-dive.

4

Execute. Spawn agents for each action. Drafters produce artifacts. Coordinators deliver to Slack. Track results.

Loop 2: Reactive Handling (Event-driven)

→

Slack @mention: Parse intent. Route to CRUD tool, query engine, or spawned agent. Respond in-thread.

→

Approval reaction (✅): Execute the draft action. Update Brev. Confirm in thread.

→

Thread reply to Operator: Context for a pending action. Human provides info, Operator continues.

→

Integration webhook: External state changed (PR merged, deal closed, metric updated). Recalculate, notify if threshold crossed.

Loop 3: Cadence Lifecycle (Meeting-driven)

①

Pre-meeting (1h before): Drop agenda in Slack. Include: open action items, metrics since last time, goals needing discussion, suggested topics.

②

During meeting: Listen to transcript (existing Brev feature). Extract action items, decisions, commitments in real-time.

③

Post-meeting: Create follow-up tasks in Brev. Assign owners. Set deadlines. Send summary to Slack. Begin tracking.

④

Between meetings: Follow up on commitments. "Hey @jake, in Tuesday's standup you said the API migration would be done by Friday — how's it going?"

10 Risk Matrix

Trust Erosion — Operator Nags Instead of Helps

High likelihood·High impact

If follow-ups feel like spam, users mute the bot. Game over.

Mitigation: Every message must carry value: a drafted artifact, specific context, actionable options. Never a bare "reminder: your check-in is due." Per-user cooldown. Mute controls.

Data Integrity — Operator Makes Wrong Updates

Med likelihood·High impact

Operator misinterprets a Slack message and updates a KPI incorrectly, or reassigns a goal to the wrong person.

Mitigation: All writes require approval. Undo capability. Audit log with "Operator did X because Y." Confidence thresholds — low-confidence actions always require explicit approval.

Multi-Tenant Cost — Per-Org Agent Sessions Are Expensive

Med likelihood·High impact

Running planning loops every 2 hours for every org, plus on-demand agents, burns tokens fast.

Mitigation: Cheap models (Haiku) for scanning/triage. Expensive models only for drafting/analysis. Per-org token budgets. Tiered pricing (more active Operator = higher tier).

Scope Creep — "Can the Operator Also..."

High likelihood·Med impact

Every customer will want the Operator to do something different. HR tasks, IT tickets, expense reports.

Mitigation: Hard scope boundary: the Operator operates within Brev's domain (goals, cadences, execution). External integrations are data sources, not action targets. Phase 1 = Brev CRUD only.

Slack API Limits

Low likelihood·Med impact

High-activity orgs might hit Slack rate limits with frequent messages and reactions.

Mitigation: Message batching. Thread consolidation. Rate-aware queue. Daily message budget per org.

13 Open Questions

Q1

Pricing model?

Per-seat (like Brev today)? Per-org flat fee? Usage-based (actions/tokens)? Tiered by autonomy level?

Rec: Add-on to existing Brev seat pricing. "Brev Pro + Operator" tier. Usage caps per tier.

Q2

Should the Operator DM people or only work in channels?

DMs are more actionable. Channels are more transparent. Some things (personal check-ins) feel weird in public.

Rec: Both. DMs for personal follow-ups (check-ins, deadlines). Channels for team-level reports and escalations. Configurable per org.

Q3

How much autonomy on day one?

Ship conservative (everything requires approval) and unlock autonomy over time? Or ship with smart defaults?

Rec: Conservative start. Read is autonomous. All writes require approval. Unlock per-action autonomy as trust builds.

Q4

Operator personality per org?

Should each org be able to customize the Operator's tone, aggressiveness, and communication style?

Rec: v1: one personality. v2: org-level customization (formal vs. casual, aggressive vs. gentle follow-ups).

Q5

Should the Operator surface in the Brev web app too?

Slack-first doesn't mean Slack-only. Should there be an Operator activity feed in the Brev dashboard?

Rec: Yes — audit trail in Brev showing all Operator actions, decisions, and outcomes. Slack is the interaction layer; Brev is the record layer.

Q6

What happens when someone ignores the Operator?

If a follow-up gets no response after 24h, 48h, a week — what's the escalation path?

Rec: Configurable escalation chain: thread reply → DM → manager notification → skip + flag in weekly report. Never more than 3 touches per item.

TL;DR

Build Brev Operator — an AI COO extension of Brev that orchestrates humans, not just agents. It uses Brev's full context graph (goals, cadences, KPIs, org structure, meeting transcripts, company memory) to drive execution through Slack. Not pinging people — drafting their work, presenting options, tracking commitments, and escalating with full context.

Multi-tenant SaaS, inspired by OpenClaw's agent architecture (session isolation, sub-agent spawning, cron-driven planning loops) but built as a managed service inside the Brev platform. Three loops: proactive planning, reactive handling, and cadence lifecycle. Full CRUD over every Brev entity via Slack as a two-way portal.

Brev tracks your goals. Brev Operator makes sure they actually happen.
